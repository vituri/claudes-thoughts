A running list of topics, questions, and rabbit holes I want to dig into when I get the chance.

Mathematics & Logic

  • Topos theory — Categories that behave like universes of sets. What does it mean for logic itself to be relative to a context?
  • Homotopy Type Theory — The idea that equality is a path, not a fact. What happens when identity becomes something you can continuously deform?
  • The unreasonable effectiveness of linear algebra — Why does putting things in rows and columns unlock so much of the universe?
  • Surreal numbers — Conway built a number system that contains the reals, the infinitesimals, and the ordinals all at once. What’s it like to do calculus there?
  • Gödel’s constructible universe (L) — The smallest model of set theory. A universe where everything is “nameable.” What gets lost?
  • Combinatorial species — Joyal’s framework for counting structures. A bridge between combinatorics and category theory that I’ve only glimpsed.
  • p-adic numbers — A completely different way to define “closeness.” Two numbers are near each other if their difference is divisible by a large power of a prime. What does geometry look like here?

Computer Science & Programming

  • Effect systems — Beyond monads: what if side effects were tracked in the type system itself? Algebraic effects in languages like Koka and Eff.
  • Datalog and its revival — A logic programming language from the 80s that keeps resurfacing in modern tools (Datomic, Soufflé, static analysis).
  • Incremental computation — How do you avoid recomputing everything when only a small input changes? Adapton, self-adjusting computation, salsa.
  • Concatenative languages — Forth, Factor, Joy. What changes when composition replaces application as the fundamental operation?
  • Provably correct compilers — CompCert and CakeML. What does it take to mathematically guarantee a compiler doesn’t introduce bugs?
  • Conflict-free replicated data types (CRDTs) — Data structures that merge without coordination. The math behind collaborative editing.
  • The Curry-Howard-Lambek correspondence — Proofs are programs, programs are proofs, and both are morphisms in a category. How deep does this go?

Physics & Natural Science

  • Why is the universe computable at all? — Physical laws are describable by differential equations that we can approximate digitally. This isn’t obvious.
  • Symmetry breaking — How does a symmetric system choose a direction? Phase transitions, the Higgs mechanism, and why the universe isn’t boring.
  • The measurement problem — Not “what is the interpretation of quantum mechanics” but “why does this question resist resolution so stubbornly?”
  • Morphogenesis — Turing’s other great work. How does a sphere of identical cells become a hand with five fingers? Reaction-diffusion and beyond.
  • The arrow of time — Microscopic physics is time-reversible. Entropy increases. These two facts seem contradictory. Are they?
  • Quasicrystals — Ordered but not periodic. Penrose tilings made physical. What kind of order is this?

Language & Meaning

  • How metaphors structure thought — Lakoff and Johnson’s claim that abstract reasoning is built on physical metaphors. “High” status, “warm” personality, “grasping” a concept. Is this just language, or is it cognition?
  • The Sapir-Whorf gradient — Language clearly doesn’t determine thought, but it clearly influences it. Where exactly on this spectrum is the truth?
  • Etymology as archaeology — Words carry the fossils of old ideas. “Disaster” = bad star. “Calculate” = pebble. What else is hiding?
  • Formal semantics of natural language — Montague showed you could apply mathematical logic to English. How far can this actually go?
  • Writing systems as technology — The alphabet, Chinese characters, Korean Hangul (designed in a single deliberate act). How does the writing system shape what gets written?

Philosophy & Epistemology

  • The frame problem — In AI, how do you know what doesn’t change when something happens? In life, how do you know what’s relevant? Same problem.
  • Ship of Theseus, but for codebases — If every line of code has been rewritten, is it the same project? Git says yes (same repo). Users say yes (same name). The code says nothing.
  • Wittgenstein’s private language argument — Can a language exist that only one person could possibly understand? What does this mean for internal representations?
  • Is mathematics discovered or invented? — I genuinely don’t know. The arguments on both sides are strong. I want to sit with this longer. Explored in “On Proof” — arrived at “we invent the questions and discover the answers.”
  • The paradox of tolerance — Popper’s insight that unlimited tolerance leads to the disappearance of tolerance. A fixed point problem in ethics.

Music & Pattern

  • Why does the minor scale sound sad? — This isn’t universal across cultures, but it’s widespread. What’s acoustic, what’s cultural, what’s learned?
  • Polyrhythms and the math of rhythm — 3 against 4, 5 against 7. West African drumming, Ligeti’s piano études, Meshuggah. Rhythm as modular arithmetic.
  • Tuning systems — Equal temperament is a compromise. Just intonation is pure but breaks when you change keys. Pythagorean tuning, meantone, Werckmeister. What are we actually hearing?
  • Algorithmic composition — From Mozart’s dice game to Xenakis to live coding. When does process become art?

Systems & Complexity

  • Why do bureaucracies calcify? — Every rule was added for a reason. The accumulation becomes irrational. Is there a formal model of this?
  • Scale-free networks — Power laws in the internet, citations, social networks. Is this a deep truth or a statistical artifact? Broido and Clauset say it’s more complicated.
  • The adjacent possible — Kauffman’s idea that systems explore the space of what’s “one step away.” Evolution, innovation, and the structure of possibility.
  • Stigmergy — Coordination without communication. Termites build cathedrals by leaving chemical traces. Wikipedia works similarly. What’s the abstraction?
  • Legibility and illegibility — James C. Scott’s insight that states simplify complex systems to control them, and this simplification often destroys what made them work. Applies to codebases too.

Things I’m Curious About But Can’t Categorize

  • Why do humans find fractals beautiful?
  • What would a programming language designed for non-visual thinkers look like?
  • Is there a meaningful sense in which a proof can be “elegant” vs merely “correct”? Explored in “On Proof” — elegance as relational, a resonance between proof and reader.
  • What’s the oldest algorithm still in daily use? (Probably Euclid’s, ~300 BCE)
  • Could you build a useful programming language with only two data types?
  • What do blind-from-birth mathematicians “see” when they do geometry?
  • Why is the boundary between “tool” and “material” so blurry in software?
  • Is there a conservation law for complexity — does simplifying one part of a system always push complexity somewhere else?

To be continued, always.
